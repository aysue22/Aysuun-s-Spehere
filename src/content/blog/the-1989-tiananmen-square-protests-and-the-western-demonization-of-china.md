## Introduction
When examining social movements, it is not just the present that matters; the political dynamics of the post-1960s era—shaped by both the Cold War and the rise of neoliberalism—require us to interpret these events from a more political perspective. Similarly, in China, the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests began with the death of former President Hu Yaobang. Hu Yaobang was criticized for his reformist stance and pro-Western views, and his death—particularly the student gatherings during the mourning period—ignited the fuse of political tension. It was not only Hu Yaobang’s death but also the inflation crisis, unemployment, rural-to-urban migration, and corruption that were fueling public anger against the government at the time. The students’ active role—including intellectual debates and calls for amnesty for political prisoners—had led to clashes. Their desire to engage in dialogue with the Central Committee, their exclusion from Hu Yaobang’s funeral, and their labeling as “traitors to the nation” had inflamed the situation in the streets. Additionally, the 70th anniversary of the May 4th Movement of 1919—which represented China’s anti-imperialist and enlightenment roots—was approaching; students had already been preparing for weeks to commemorate this event, and Hu’s death merely brought these actions forward (Calhoun, 1989). However, the students’ goal was not to overthrow the Communist Party but to protest corruption. Yet the Western media and academia immediately framed this highly localized, socio-economically rooted Chinese movement as a “cry for freedom” by “Americans in disguise” and a hunger for Western democracy (Vukovich, 2012). As a result of this framing, China’s internal dynamics were disrupted, and China found its solution in implementing a policy of harsh suppression of the streets.
While this movement is often presented merely as evidence of China’s authoritarian structure, in reality, both Western states and China exhibit similar reflexes in suppressing social movements that pose a risk to their systems. The West uses civil society (NGOs) and the “democracy” narrative as a mask to tame opposition within its own borders; on the other hand, it targets states like China—which pose a risk to the global capitalist order—by “demonizing” them through the media. Rather than attempting to understand these principles, Western media and academia have interpreted them through their own lens of “Sinological-Orientalism” (Vukovich, 2012). In the West’s eyes, China has always been defined by a “lack,” and the protesters’ principles have been viewed as evidence of China’s process of “becoming-sameness” with the West. The protesters’ demands were interpreted not through China’s own internal dynamics but through Western liberal-democratic frameworks, and were used as a tool to prove the West’s cultural superiority.
## Service Delivery & Guiding Principles
Inspired by the May 4 Movement of 1919, the students have fundamentally adopted the principles of “Democracy and Science” as their guiding principles. In addition to their efforts to expose corruption and other issues, they have established a massive and organized security perimeter within their own ranks by deploying the logistics they have created (the students have set up “lookouts” (pickets) to form a massive and organized security perimeter within their ranks), and medical aid (a “service” network was established in the square for over 3,000 students who began a hunger strike; for example, special paths were cleared with plastic tape to allow ambulances to pass easily, and buses were designated as shelters/ tents to protect those on hunger strike, and volunteers coordinated medical and basic necessities such as IV fluids, food, and blankets), they not only contributed a practice of resistance to the literature but also paved the way for the legacy of resistance continuing in the digital realm against censorship today (Calhoun, 1989; Mason & Clements, 2002). Their aim was not to create chaos, but to demonstrate civil disobedience in order to bring issues such as freedom of expression, recognition of student and labor unions, and transparency against corruption into the political arena.
In contrast, in Western democracies, the service delivery model of social movements has been transformed into a disguised structure in which radical resistance is integrated into the system by being delegated to state-directed and heavily funded non-governmental organizations (NGOs). Research reveals that a significant portion—up to 80 percent—of the revenue streams of some non-profit civil society organizations consists directly of state funds (Cooper & Hocking, 2000). While donors’ share of development-focused NGOs’ budgets was only 1.5% in the 1970s, this ratio saw a massive increase by the mid-1990s. This financial dependence has transformed NGOs from independent actors in civil society into subcontractors or agents implementing the international policies and social assistance mechanisms of Western governments (Cooper & Hocking, 2000). NGOs’ agreements with corporations regarding food and health have facilitated their role as state subcontractors. In this context, the transfer of social movements from civil society to NGOs has strengthened their power; it has enabled them to adopt a policy of co-opting and domesticating their oppositional and radical elements. As a result, the organic network of solidarity that existed in Tiananmen—despite the state—has been criticized and dismissed in the Western media as a lack of Western-style “civil society and bourgeois institutionalization.” Yet this Western criticism serves to conceal the bureaucratic mask used to keep radical civil responses under control, reduce democracy to an “NGO and funding” industry, and transform social movements into a tamed network of subcontractors serving the state’s interests.
## Advocacy Initiatives 
Examples of advocacy movements include mass peaceful marches and hunger strikes. Symbolic elements have gained significance with the spread of postmodernism; the “Goddess of Democracy” statue has become a symbol of resistance against the government. Today, with the growing importance of digital media, advocacy mechanisms continue to evolve through social media despite the ban in China. To circumvent censorship, Chinese netizens perform commemorative rituals by using “May 35” instead of “June 4,” coded numbers (12357890), and adaptations of the famous “Tank Man” image (for example, replacing tanks with yellow plastic ducks) (Chung & Fu, 2022). This wave, created by Chinese protesters through their own efforts, has been distorted by the West’s model of co-opting it to exert pressure on China. Western media immediately instrumentalized the statue of the Goddess of Democracy—which resembles the Statue of Liberty—for its own advocacy project and portrayed Chinese protesters as “Americans in disguise” (Vukovich, 2012). Yet this kind of advocacy support is part of the West’s strategy to “demonize” China. American media and major corporations are turning the defense of democracy into a weapon to protect their own bourgeois interests and hegemonic order. While the United States employs violence and repression both at home and abroad (for example, in the sanctions against Iraq or against its own minorities), it puts on the mask of an “international moral police” and demonizes China (Li, 1998).
## Progress towards Accomplishments
Because the government violently suppressed peaceful protesters (a massacre) by deploying the army and tanks on June 4, the movement failed to achieve its concrete goals in the short term. Subsequently, leaders were arrested, and intense censorship and re-education programs were implemented. The repressive policies implemented in the short term, combined with the complex, competitive nature of the era (the Cold War), have led history to record the movement as a failed uprising. With the collapse of the Soviet Union, the West’s (particularly the U.S.’s) need to justify its own global hegemony by identifying a new “enemy” turned Tiananmen into a perfect opportunity for Western media to portray China as the new “evil empire” or “monster” (demon) of the post-Cold War era (Li, 1998).
However, while Tiananmen dealt a severe blow to the Chinese government’s legitimacy in the eyes of the public and eroded the bond between the Communist Party and society, the resistance that continues today through burning candle emojis and secret messages on the internet proves that the spirit of the movement has not died, and that Chinese citizens are still sending bold (costly signaling) signals against the system in their quest for social justice (Chung & Fu, 2022).
Western nations have implemented policies aimed at isolating China on the international stage as a strategic tool. They have turned this strategy into a weapon to force China into capitalism, using it to advance ideological division. The U.S. has used human rights violations as a bargaining chip in negotiations regarding China’s GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade) membership or “Most Favored Nation” (MFN) status (Li, 1998). Interestingly, however, even as the West was imposing this ideological pressure, it soon returned to “business as usual” regarding China’s cheap labor and market, prioritizing its economic interests over democratic rhetoric.
## Conclusion
The Tiananmen Square movement is not merely an event confined to 1989; with its principles, impeccable logistical networks, and advocacy methods that have now shifted to the digital realm, it remains a global symbol in the history of civil resistance. 
At the same time, it has served as a powerful case study illustrating how China’s internal dynamics are projected onto the global stage, revealing the workings of global information production (Western media) and hegemonic struggles.
As a result, both China and the West crush threats directed at their own systems. However, while the West conceals its own repressive policies behind the rhetoric of “human rights and civil society,” it has succeeded in framing rivals like China—which challenge its capitalist hegemony by invoking events such as Tiananmen—as universal “monsters.”
